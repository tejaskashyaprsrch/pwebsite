sim_01 — voterSim.py

Two-Party Voter Simulation

The voterSim.py script serves as the baseline for my research, simulating a standard US two-party election. It utilizes a 10-dimensional ideology space where voters are generated into four categories—Democrat, Republican, Center, and Cross—with distribution ratios modeled after 2025 Pew Research data. Each dimension represents a specific policy area, from the economy to civil rights, allowing for a nuanced representation of political alignment beyond a simple left-right spectrum.

To measure the effectiveness of the system, I use an "alienation" metric (the inverse of happiness), which calculates the Euclidean distance between a voter's 10D position and the position of the party they vote for. The simulation runs multiple trials to account for ideological noise, providing a statistical average of how well the two major parties represent the diverse interests of the American electorate.

Voter Shares (Based on Pew Research, 2025)

Results from the two-party simulation consistently showed a high level of "collective alienation," particularly for voters in the Center and Cross categories. While one party usually secures a narrow victory based on slight shifts in turnout noise, the total happiness metric remains relatively low because large segments of the population are forced to choose the "lesser of two evils," voting for a party that may still be quite distant from their actual 10D ideological coordinate.

sim_02 — voterThirdPartySim.py

Third-Party Dynamics

Building on the baseline, voterThirdPartySim.py introduces an adaptive third party. Initially starting at a neutral [0]*10 position, this party is designed to "learn" from each election cycle, shifting its ideological coordinates toward the average position of the voters who supported it. This simulation explores the viability of a centrist or alternative movement within a rigid two-party framework.

A key feature of this model is the "Trump effect" variable, which simulates how increased polarization boosts turnout for the two major parties. It also factors in a "fear factor"—the psychological barrier that prevents voters from supporting a third party even if they prefer its platform, out of fear that doing so will hand the election to the major party they dislike the most.

Voter Shares (with Third Party)
Democrat35.0%
Republican35.5%
Center25.5%
Third Party1.68%
Cross2.32%

Negative partisanship is modeled through a specific penalty formula: distDem -= (repExtreme * 0.4). Conceptually, this means that as the opposing party becomes more extreme, a voter’s perceived distance to their "own" party effectively shrinks—not because their party moved closer to them, but because their fear of the opposition drives them into a defensive loyalist position. This effectively traps the electorate in a two-party loop regardless of personal happiness.

In these simulations, the third party often struggled to gain significant win counts due to the aforementioned negative partisanship penalties. However, its evolution was fascinating: over 100 elections, the third party would "drift" to find ideological gaps left by the two major parties. While it rarely won, its presence significantly lowered the collective alienation of the "Center" voters, proving that even a non-winning third party adds value to democratic representation.

sim_03 — claudeElectionModel.py

Multi-Party Optimal Count Model

The claudeElectionModel.py is my most robust simulation, designed to answer the core question of my research: "What is the optimal number of parties?" Unlike previous models, this one spreads parties across the ideological spectrum using a noise epsilon (ε) to ensure realistic, non-perfect placement. The simulation is massive in scale, running 50 trials for each party count (from 2 to 20), with 200 elections per trial and 2,000 voters per election.

Model Parameters
Voters per election2,000
Elections per trial200
Trials per party count50
Party range tested2–20
Position noise (ε)0.02

I define "collective alienation" as the average Euclidean distance between every voter in the system and the ideological position of the election's winner. This is a vital measure of democratic quality; a lower number indicates that the winning party actually reflects the will and values of the people. In a 10D space, this distance captures nuances that a simple majority vote often ignores.

The results were striking: the 3-party system emerged as the optimal configuration. Moving from 2 to 3 parties caused the single largest drop in collective alienation. While adding even more parties (4, 5, or 10) continued to lower alienation slightly, the "noise" and fragmentation increased, and the marginal gains became negligible. The 3-party setup provides the perfect balance—it offers enough variety to represent the center while remaining stable enough to avoid the total ideological chaos of a 20-party system.

sim_04 — voterMultiPartyExample.py

Multi-Party Example Simulation

The voterMultiPartyExample.py script is a refactored, modular version of the simulation used to demonstrate how easily the model can be expanded. It introduces a formal Party class, allowing for individual party traits like "turnout boost" or "issue weights." This script was used to explore specific "what-if" scenarios—such as a system with four parties of equal strength—to observe how the average difference between the top two finishers changes as the field becomes more crowded.
